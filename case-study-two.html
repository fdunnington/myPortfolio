CONTENT DESIGN DRUPAL GDS

End-to-End Service Design

Role: Content Design Lead

Understanding, structuring, and simplifying with GDS

We were working with an information hub built in WordPress that focused on the criminal justice system. The existing website was a disjointed knowledge base; content was fragmented, incomplete, and difficult to navigate. Our task was to restructure, rewrite, and migrate the platform to LocalGov Drupal, building in line with GDS standards and WCAG accessibility guidelines.

Information about the criminal justice system is widely available, but often scattered, inconsistent, and rarely comprehensive. We decided to reframe the project as service development rather than a website restructure, shifting the focus to solving problems across the user experience rather than just presenting information.

This case study focuses on how we approached the work, showing the processes, methods, and reasoning behind our decisions.

STAGE 1: Establish what users need

We started by clarifying the service goals — what were we aiming for and why? We looked at how users actually used the existing website, and at wider trends in how people search for and access criminal justice information. This allowed us to compare service goals with how people actually used the website, and to identify gaps in existing content as well as content redundancies.

We gathered this data using processes like:

  • Interviews with internal experts and service leads – defining goals, assumptions, and success from across the team.
  • User interviews and contextual research – understanding real behaviours, needs, and pain points on the website and in the broader context of the criminal justice system.
  • Journey mapping – comparing the intended website experience with the real user experience to make gaps and issues visible.
  • Affinity mapping – grouping research findings into patterns and themes to identify clear user needs.

STAGE 2: Do less

Information hubs often fail by trying to provide everything at once. To avoid another ‘content dump’, we used data and search trends to identify the information most important to our users. We used the service goals to prioritise these findings, refining them to the essential elements: key concepts (what users care about), core actions (what they need to do), and the relationships between them.

We used analytics from existing content to understand how users were already navigating the system. These existing user journeys gave us clear starting points for how users thought about and moved through the content. Techniques like object mapping and entity relationship diagrams helped us define core objects in the system, and clarify relationships and dependencies.

The service blueprint we built allowed us to move from abstract data to a task-led content structure. We adopted proven GDS design patterns and consulted with other government service providers to find frameworks that had worked well in similar scenarios. This allowed us to build a fit-for-purpose and scalable structure.

STAGE 3: Do the hard work to make it simple

Our goal throughout was to provide a comprehensive, accessible, and effective service. Legal jargon and procedural complexity can make the criminal justice system difficult to navigate. We worked to remove that complexity, replacing it with clear, plain language and simple steps.

We organised information into a clear hierarchy, replacing dense blocks of text with step-by-step guides and intuitive navigation patterns. By using established patterns, a consistent design and strategic internal linking, we ensured that users could find what they needed and understand what to do next without losing their way. This approach allowed us to simplify a complex journey into a manageable experience, while maintaining the absolute accuracy required for legal information.

We worked with WCAG and GDS guidelines in mind, building accessibility into the bones of the service. Processes were documented to enable future teams to refine, build and scale the project in a way that encouraged consistency and continuity, whilst allowing for adaptation.
