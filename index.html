Visual Design

Everything about how it looks

What visual design involves:

Creating the visual style of a product or website, including layout, colour palettes, typography, imagery, and iconography. It's about establishing a consistent look and feel that communicates the brand and makes content easy to digest.

Visual design can range from high-level style guides to detailed mockups, depending on the project’s scope and complexity.

When and why it's important:

Visual design shapes how users perceive a product. It builds trust, reinforces brand identity, and improves comprehension of content. Investing time in this stage ensures that design choices are intentional and accessible, avoiding rework later in development. Strong visual design also supports usability by guiding users’ attention to the most important information.

Structural Design

The blueprint, and how it all fits together

What structural design involves:

Defining the hierarchy and layout of content, navigation, and interactive elements. This includes sitemaps, content grouping, and functional flows that show how different pieces relate to each other.

Structural design can range from rough sketches of page layouts to more detailed diagrams of user journeys, depending on complexity and project needs.

When and why it's important:

A clear structure helps users understand a product or website quickly and navigate it efficiently. It prevents confusion, reduces cognitive load, and ensures that content and features are discoverable.

Spending time on structural design upfront makes development smoother and allows testing of user flows before committing to build.

UX Writing

Words really do matter

What UX writing involves:

Writing clear, concise, and helpful text that appears within a product, from buttons and labels to notifications and error messages. The goal is to make interactions intuitive and reinforce the product’s tone of voice.

UX writing can range from small copy snippets to detailed content frameworks, depending on the number of user touchpoints and complexity of interactions.

When and why it's important:

Words are part of the user interface. Well-written copy reduces confusion, guides actions, and improves overall experience.

Investing in UX writing ensures messages are consistent, understandable, and accessible, and can prevent unnecessary frustration for users. Clear copy at this stage also helps avoid last-minute rewrites during development.

Web Accessibility

Reaching as many people as possible

What web accessibility involves:

Ensuring that websites and products can be accessed and used by people with a wide range of abilities, including those with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive impairments. This includes considerations such as colour contrast, keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, and clear content structure.

Accessibility measures can range from basic checks to detailed audits and testing with assistive technologies, depending on the project’s scale and audience.

When and why it's important:

Accessible design ensures equal access to information and functionality, which is both ethically responsible and often legally required. It improves usability for all users, not just those with disabilities, and can prevent costly fixes after launch.

Prioritising accessibility from the start allows for a smoother, more inclusive design and development process.
